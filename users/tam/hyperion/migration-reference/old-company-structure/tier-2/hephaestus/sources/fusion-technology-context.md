## Key Information

### Negative Triangularity in Fusion

**What It Is**:
Negative triangularity refers to the plasma cross-sectional shape in a tokamak reactor. Traditional tokamaks use positive triangularity (D-shaped plasma), but negative triangularity reverses this shape.

**Potential Benefits**:
- Enhanced stability and performance
- Can suppress edge instabilities (ELMs - Edge Localized Modes)
- May enable more compact, cost-effective fusion reactors
- Improved confinement properties

### Spherical Tokamak

**Design**:
Spherical tokamaks have a more compact, spherical shape compared to traditional "donut-shaped" tokamaks. Lower aspect ratio (closer to a sphere than a torus).

**Advantages**:
- Potentially more cost-effective
- Better plasma stability
- More compact design (lower capital costs)

### Industry Context

**SMART Tokamak** (University of Seville + Princeton):
- First spherical tokamak fully exploring negative triangularity
- Generated first plasma in January 2025
- Global research collaboration

**Research Status**:
- Negative triangularity is an active area of fusion research
- Multiple institutions exploring this approach (Princeton, UK's MAST-U, SMART)
- Still experimental, not commercially proven

### Competitive Landscape Context

**Major Fusion Approaches**:
1. **Magnetic Confinement** (tokamaks, stellarators)
   - Commonwealth Fusion Systems (ARC reactor, positive triangularity)
   - TAE Technologies (field-reversed configuration)
   - Tokamak Energy (spherical tokamaks)

2. **Inertial Confinement** (laser fusion)
   - NIF (National Ignition Facility)
   - First Light Fusion

3. **Alternative Approaches**
   - Helion Energy (magneto-inertial)
   - General Fusion (magnetized target fusion)

**AI/Software for Fusion**:
- Multiple companies developing plasma control and simulation software
- Machine learning for plasma physics becoming important enabler
- Software-focused companies emerging as fusion ecosystem expands
